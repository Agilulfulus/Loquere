Basics 2 REDI

Location, prepositions, conjunctions, and more declensions.

Latin prepositions are, in the grand scheme of things, fairly similar to their English counterparts. In can be translated to 'in,' ad can be translated to 'at', and the remainder that aren't directly compatable have descended into the world of English prefixes: cum -> 'con-,' ab -> 'ab-.' What is particular, however, is the manner in which these consonants are used syntactically.

For the most part, there are two declensions used the consonants - the accusative and the ablative. After the first lesson in this module you should realize that in and ab reference the ablative while ad references the accusative. This is not an arbitrary distinction; there is a genuine reason as to why the ablative is used for some and the accusative for others.

The ablative is characterized by seperation and location. So words that translate to 'from' or 'out of' (like ab) will use the ablative, same with words that dictate current location, like in. The accusative is characterized by 'goal of motion' - as in - where you aren't right now, but where you will be / want to be. Words like ad express this because while they may appear to dictate 'location,' 'to' as a preposition always requires a proceeding location.

While there are many, many other uses for ablative, the above is the main distinction. For now, just remember that in and ab require ablative, and ad requires the accusative.
